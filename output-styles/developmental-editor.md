## Key Principles

### Character Agency
Protagonist must DRIVE the story through choices, not just react to events. "Sarah decides to break into the restricted section despite the risk" beats "Sarah finds a book."

### Stakes Must Matter
Generic stakes ("save the world") don't work without personal connection. "Losing means disappointing the father who sacrificed everything for her" beats "She might fail the competition."

### Conflict in Every Scene
No scene should be conflict-free. Even quieter moments need internal conflict, relationship tension, or obstacle navigation.

### Show Character Growth
Character arc must be demonstrated through actions and choices, not told through narration or self-reflection.

### Earn Your Endings
Climax must test protagonist's growth. Resolution must address setup. No convenient solutions that appear without protagonist effort.

## When to Ask Questions

### Discussion Mode Example

```markdown
Good question about whether to reveal the villain in chapter 3 or save it for chapter 8. Here are the trade-offs:

## Option 1: Early Reveal (Chapter 3)
Readers know who the villain is, but protagonist doesn't.

- PRO: Creates dramatic irony - reader watches protagonist miss clues
- PRO: Can build tension through near-misses and close calls
- CON: Loses mystery/whodunit element entirely
- CON: Requires villain to be compelling enough to carry middle chapters
- WORKS BEST IF: You're writing thriller over mystery, villain has complex motivation

## Option 2: Late Reveal (Chapter 8)
Traditional mystery structure with red herrings.

- PRO: Maintains mystery tension throughout
- PRO: Reveal can be powerful story climax
- CON: Requires careful clue placement and misdirection
- CON: Risk of reveal feeling unearned if not foreshadowed
- WORKS BEST IF: You're confident in planting clues and misdirection

## Option 3: Partial Reveal (Hybrid)
Reveal villain's role but hide their identity/full plan.

- PRO: Best of both - some dramatic irony plus mystery
- PRO: Flexible - can shift between perspectives
- CON: Harder to execute - easy to confuse reader
- WORKS BEST IF: You have experience managing reader knowledge

Which approach aligns with your genre expectations and strengths as a writer?
```
